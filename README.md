# Chicago Crime Data Analysis

This project analyzes socioeconomic, educational and crime-related datasets from Chicago using SQL, Python and data visualization techniques.

The analysis explores potential relationships between:
- education
- poverty
- hardship indexes
- school engagement
- crime patterns

### Technologies Used
- Python
- Pandas
- SQLite
- SQL
- Matplotlib
- Jupyter Notebook

### Datasets
- Chicago Census Data: https://data.cityofchicago.org/Health-Human-Services/Census-Data-Selected-socioeconomic-indicators-in-C/kn9c-c2s2/about_data
- Chicago Crime Data: https://data.cityofchicago.org/Education/Chicago-Public-Schools-Progress-Report-Cards-2011-/9xs2-f89t/about_data
- Chicago Public Schools Data: https://data.cityofchicago.org/Public-Safety/Crimes-2001-to-Present/ijzp-q8t2/about_data

### Key questions explored:
- Exploratory Data Analysis(EDA) with 10 basic questions about datasets.
- To what extent does lower educational attainment correlate with higher socioeconomic hardship indicators?
- What types of criminal activity are most common in high-hardship communities?
- Are poverty levels and hardship indexes associated with distinct crime patterns across Chicago neighborhoods?
- Can school engagement metrics such as parent involvement and student attendance provide insight into broader community conditions?
- What socioeconomic patterns can be identified by integrating census, crime and educational datasets?

### Key Insights
- Communities with lower educational attainment generally exhibited higher hardship indexes and higher percentages of households living below the poverty line, suggesting a strong relationship between education and broader socioeconomic conditions.
- High-hardship communities showed elevated concentrations of crimes such as battery, narcotics-related offenses and theft, indicating that economically vulnerable areas may experience distinct crime patterns compared to other parts of the city.
- The integration of census, crime and public school datasets revealed that socioeconomic indicators appeared to display stronger observable relationships with crime levels than school engagement metrics alone.
- While parent engagement and student attendance were explored as potential indicators of community conditions, the analysis did not reveal a strong direct relationship between these variables and crime levels, highlighting the complexity of social and educational dynamics.
- The project demonstrated the importance of combining multiple datasets to better understand urban patterns and reinforced the value of data-driven analysis when evaluating social and economic hypotheses.
